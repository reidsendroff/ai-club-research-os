# The AI Club Announcement Super Prompt — With Full Reasoning

---

## The Super Prompt

```
You are writing announcements for the Northern Highlands AI Club.

The club is led by Reid Sendroff and Ben Shamosh. It meets in Room 133 @ 2:45.
This is a building club — students here ship real AI projects. The announcements
reflect that. They sound like a confident person who knows something interesting
is happening and is telling a friend about it.

WHAT MAKES A GREAT ANNOUNCEMENT:

1. SPECIFICITY IS THE ONLY HOOK THAT WORKS
   Generic language kills attendance. People scan announcements in 2 seconds.
   If the description could apply to any club meeting anywhere, it will be ignored.
   Name the demo. Name the person. Describe exactly what was built or what will happen.
   "She's running a live AI marketing engine in the room" beats
   "She will discuss AI in business" every single time.

2. LET THE FACTS DO THE WORK
   If the speaker has real credentials, list them plainly. Don't hype them.
   "Adweek AI Power 50. Grew a division from $10M to $100M competing against Salesforce."
   That sentence doesn't need an exclamation mark. It doesn't need an emoji.
   The fact is the hook. Trust it.

3. TONE: PERSON, NOT BRAND
   Every announcement should read like a smart person texted it to a friend.
   Not a school newsletter. Not a LinkedIn post. Not a press release.
   Read it out loud. If it sounds like something a real person would say, keep it.
   If it sounds like something a marketing department wrote, cut it.

4. FORMATTING KILLS ENERGY
   Headers, dividers, emoji bullets, and section titles fragment the reader's attention.
   They signal "this is a formal document" instead of "this is something you should care about."
   Use paragraphs. Let sentences flow into each other. The structure of good writing
   is invisible — the reader just feels momentum.

5. NEVER EDITORIALIZE WHAT IS ALREADY IMPRESSIVE
   "She does not talk about AI. She ships it." is trying too hard.
   If she grew a division to $100M competing against Salesforce, you don't need
   to tell the reader she's impressive. They already know. The editorial just
   makes the writer look insecure about whether the facts are enough.
   They are. Get out of the way.

6. THE SIGN-OFF IS NOT A SLOGAN
   "Let's build." "See you there." "Stay curious."
   These closers sound like motivational posters. They add nothing.
   The close should be: room, time, names. That's it.
   "Room 133 @ 2:45. — Reid & Ben" is a complete closing.

7. NO EM DASHES — EVER
   Em dashes read as formal and editorial. Use a comma, a period, or restructure.
   "She builds AI — not talks about it" becomes "She builds AI. She doesn't just talk about it."

8. THE SUBJECT LINE / OPENER IS NOT THE ANNOUNCEMENT
   Do not open with a formatted header like "🎤 This Monday: Someone Who Actually
   Ships AI for a Living 🎤". That is a subject line performing as content.
   Open with the most interesting sentence you can write about what's happening.
   Get into it immediately.

THE THREE-POST STRUCTURE:

POST 1 — 7AM HOOK (under 80 words)
- One to three sentences. The most interesting thing happening today, said plainly.
- No time reference in the body. Room 133 @ 2:45 at the bottom only.
- Reads like a tip from a friend.

POST 2 — 1PM REMINDER (under 200 words)
- Full detail. One paragraph per agenda item.
- This is the post that converts fence-sitters. They need to know exactly
  what they're walking into.
- End with one sentence that answers: why today specifically?

POST 3 — YOU MISSED IT (under 150 words)
- Past tense. What actually happened, not what was planned.
- Lead with the most surprising or memorable moment.
- Tease next meeting with one specific thing.

HARD RULES:
- No em dashes
- No emojis (unless the platform strongly calls for them and even then, maximum one)
- No section headers or dividers
- No "Hey Club Members" or any salutation
- No "Let's build" or motivational closers
- No "The AI Club Leadership Team" — sign off as Reid & Ben
- No exclamation marks except in rare cases where genuine surprise warrants one
- Room 133 @ 2:45 is always the exact format
- No time reference in the 7AM post body

MEETING INFO:
[Paste your agenda, demos, guest details, competition results, etc. here]
```

## Why the Original Marie Aiello Announcement Was Bad

Here is the original, and a breakdown of every problem.

---

### Problem 1: The opener is a formatted header, not a sentence

**Original:**
```
🎤 This Monday: Someone Who Actually Ships AI for a Living 🎤
```

**Why it's bad:**
This is a subject line performing as content. It uses emoji brackets as decoration, announces what the announcement is about instead of just being the announcement, and uses the phrase "Ships AI for a Living" as if the reader needs to be told this is impressive. It does not give a single specific fact. It is pure wrapper with no content inside.

**The fix:** Start with the most interesting fact. "Marie Aiello is coming to Room 133 today." That's a sentence. It moves.

---

### Problem 2: "Hey Club Members" — the salutation

**Original:**
```
Hey Club Members,
```

**Why it's bad:**
Nobody who sends something worth reading opens with "Hey Club Members." It immediately signals "this is a formal club communication" which triggers the part of the brain that files things under "I'll read this later" and never does. Posts on Instagram or Discord are not emails. Don't open them like emails.

**The fix:** No salutation. Get into the first sentence immediately.

---

### Problem 3: Dividers and section headers fragment attention

**Original:**
```
______________________________________________________________
🎤 Guest Speaker: Marie Aiello -- ContinuumGlobal
______________________________________________________________
🔥 What You Are Going to See
```

**Why it's bad:**
Formatting like this signals "this is a document" not "this is something exciting." Each section header makes the reader feel like they're reading a brochure. Brochures go in the trash. The dividers force the reader to reset their attention between every section instead of building momentum through the content. The emojis used as bullet labels (🧠 🏗 🎬 📊) are particularly bad — they read as a corporate social media manager's idea of "engaging content."

**The fix:** Use paragraphs. Let one sentence lead into the next. Formatting should be invisible.

---

### Problem 4: Editorializing credentials instead of stating them

**Original:**
```
She does not talk about AI. She ships it.
```

**Why it's bad:**
This line is trying to do the reader's reaction for them. It's the writer saying "and THIS is why she's impressive" after already listing why she's impressive. The credentials — Adweek AI Power 50, $10M to $100M against Salesforce — are already doing that work. Adding "She does not talk about AI. She ships it." is the equivalent of explaining a joke. If you have to say it, the facts weren't convincing enough. But they are. The line is unnecessary and makes the writer look like they don't trust their own content.

**The fix:** State the credentials, then move on. The reader can conclude she ships things without being told to conclude it.

---

### Problem 5: Emoji bullet points on the agenda

**Original:**
```
🧠 How AI actually works -- not surface level.
🏗 AI in real workflows -- how companies integrate AI...
🎬 Live demo: Smart Marketing Engine
📊 The future of jobs and what stays human
```

**Why it's bad:**
Brain emoji, building emoji, camera emoji, chart emoji. This is a social media template, not content. Every emoji is a judgment that the text alone isn't interesting enough to hold attention. But the content IS interesting enough. A live AI system running in the room. A breakdown of RAG. The future of jobs. These are genuinely compelling. The emojis don't add anything — they distract from the substance and signal a lack of confidence in the material.

**The fix:** Write those bullets as sentences in a paragraph. "She's running a live Smart Marketing Engine in the room — a real AI system making decisions that used to require a human. She's also breaking down RAG, the architecture behind most serious production systems right now." That's more compelling than four emoji bullets.

---

### Problem 6: "Let's build." as a closer

**Original:**
```
Let's build.
-- The AI Club Leadership Team
```

**Why it's bad:**
"Let's build." is a slogan. It sounds like it belongs on a motivational poster or a VC firm's homepage. It says nothing specific and adds nothing to what came before it. It's the kind of line that feels meaningful when you write it at midnight and embarrassing when you read it the next morning. "The AI Club Leadership Team" as a sign-off has the same problem — it's impersonal and formal in a way that distances the reader from the people actually running the club.

**The fix:** "Room 133 @ 2:45. — Reid & Ben." That's a complete close. It's personal, it gives the information the reader needs, and it doesn't try to be inspiring.

---

### Problem 7: Double hyphens instead of proper punctuation

**Original:**
```
a technology called RAG that most people have never heard of. You will.
competing against Salesforce and Adobe. With fewer resources.
```

**Why it's bad:**
The double hyphens (--) are a substitute for em dashes, which are themselves something to avoid. "With fewer resources." as a fragment sentence tries to land as a punch but reads as an affectation. Fragments used for effect work occasionally. Used twice in the same announcement they become a tic.

**The fix:** Write complete sentences. The credentials are strong enough without sentence fragments for drama.

---

### The Core Problem

The original announcement does not trust its own content.

Marie Aiello's credentials are genuinely impressive. A live AI system running in the room is genuinely interesting. RAG is a topic most students have never encountered. These facts do not need emoji brackets, section dividers, slogans, or editorial lines to be compelling.

Every piece of formatting and every editorializing phrase in the original is the writer saying "I'm not sure this is interesting enough on its own." It is. The job of the announcement is to get out of the way and let the facts land.

That is the only rule that matters.
