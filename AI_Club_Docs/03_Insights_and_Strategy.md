# AI Club — Insights & Strategic Notes

What's worked, what hasn't, and the thinking behind the decisions made in 2025–2026.

---

## What Actually Drives Attendance

**Specificity beats hype every time.**
Announcements that named the exact demo ("ChatGPT Images 2.0 — native GPT-4o image generation, text rendering finally fixed") outperformed vague ones ("we're covering AI image tools") consistently. People need to know what they're walking into before they'll commit the afternoon.

**The 1PM post is the highest-leverage touchpoint.**
Morning posts build awareness. The 1PM post converts. People who are on the fence about coming make that decision around lunch. That's when the full agenda, the detail, and the "here's what you'll miss" framing does its work.

**You Missed It posts are secret recruiting tools.**
People who weren't there read them. People who were there share them. They work as social proof and as a standing invitation. Never skip the recap post.

**Names matter more than titles.**
"Reid is showing how he built X" pulls better than "the club president will demonstrate X." Use first names. It signals authenticity.

---

## What Hasn't Worked

**Formal language kills engagement.**
Any post that sounds like it was written for a school newsletter gets ignored. "We are pleased to announce" is dead on arrival.

**Generic topic descriptions.**
"We're covering generative AI" tells nobody anything. If you can't describe the demo in one concrete sentence, the announcement isn't ready yet.

**Posting too early.**
Announcements posted the day before rarely drive the same response as same-day posts. The 7AM + 1PM same-day cadence works because urgency is real.

**Em dashes.**
They read as try-hard or corporate depending on context. Neither is the vibe. Cut them.

## The Presentation Strategy

Presentations that land follow a consistent structure regardless of topic:

1. **What changed** — Why this matters now. Not history, not background. What is new.
2. **The thing itself** — A live demo whenever possible. No slides-only presentations for technical topics.
3. **How it works** — Just enough technical depth that people understand what they're looking at.
4. **What you can build with it** — Concrete use cases. Specific, not abstract.
5. **Takeaway** — One thing the audience can do or try before the next meeting.

The worst presentations are the ones that explain what a tool is without ever showing it do something real.

## The Club's Positioning

The AI Club at Northern Highlands is not a "learn about AI" club. It's a building club. The distinction matters in how you present it, recruit for it, and run it.

**What it is:**
- A place where students ship real projects
- A community that competes nationally (Congressional App Challenge, FBLA, MIT Blueprint)
- A program that gives students access to the same tools used by professional engineers

**What it's not:**
- A passive lecture series
- A resume line for students who don't want to build anything
- A club that exists to celebrate AI rather than use it

This positioning should show up in every announcement. The implicit message is always: "if you're serious about building things, this is where you do it."

## The Three-Demo Meeting Format

The meetings that drove the most engagement had three distinct demos, each from a different person. This matters for a few reasons:

- **Variety.** Three different presenting styles, three different topics. Someone who isn't interested in one will be interested in another.
- **Distributed ownership.** When Ben, Reid, and Arjun each own a section, the meeting doesn't rise and fall on one person.
- **Pacing.** Three 15-minute demos with breaks is more engaging than one 45-minute presentation.

The best demo lineup mixes technical depth (how something works under the hood) with practical application (here's how I used it to build something) and accessibility (here's something anyone in this room can try today).

## The Email Strategy

For internal club communication and proposals to administration:

- **No em dashes.** Ever.
- **No "Thank you for your time."**
- **Get to the point in the first sentence.** Mr. Novak reads a lot of emails. The ask or the purpose should be in line one.
- **Bullet points for requests.** Make it easy to say yes to each item individually.
- **Signed from Reid and Ben**, not from "the AI Club leadership team."
- **Follow up exactly once** if no response after 5 school days.

The Room 133 email to Mr. Novak worked because it was short, the ask was clear, and it didn't try to justify everything. One page, three asks, done.

## What to Do When Attendance Drops

It happens. Here's the playbook:

1. **Post the You Missed It for the previous meeting more prominently.** FOMO is the fastest re-engagement tool.
2. **Tease the next meeting earlier.** If people know a week in advance what's coming, they plan for it.
3. **Recruit through the demo.** If a member built something cool, have them post about it independently. The club gets the residual.
4. **Check the topic.** Generic topics pull generic attendance. If the demo isn't specific enough, the announcement can't save it.

## The Long Game

The goal isn't a great spring semester. The goal is a club that's still running well three years after Reid and Ben graduate.

Everything built into the strategic plan — scrum format, project teams, succession protocol, distributed leadership — exists to make that possible. The announcements, the demos, the open lab — those are what make people show up now. The structure is what makes the club survive.

The two things are not in conflict. Build the culture first. The institution follows.
