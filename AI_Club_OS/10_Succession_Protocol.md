# 10 — Succession Protocol

---

## The Premise

Every strong organization eventually loses the people who built it. The question is not whether that will happen — it's whether the organization survives it well or badly.

The AI Club survives it well if, six months after the current leadership graduates, a new person opens this document and can run the club at the same level without needing to call Reid or Ben to ask how anything works.

That is the only test that matters.

---

## The Succession Timeline

Succession is a process, not an event. It starts in February and ends in June.

### February — Identify Candidates

**Co-Presidents review:**
- Who is attending consistently (75%+ attendance)
- Who has demonstrated ownership of something (led a project, run a scrum, organized anything)
- Who has expressed interest in leadership (explicitly or through behavior)
- Who is not a senior (or if a senior, are they staying local for college and potentially available to advise)

**Action:** Co-Presidents and VP/COOs discuss candidate list informally. No formal nominations yet. Just awareness.

---

### March — Conversations

**Each current officer has a 1:1 with 1–2 members they believe could take their role.**

This is not a formal interview. It is a conversation:
> "We're thinking about next year's leadership. I wanted to talk to you because I think you'd be good at [role]. What do you think? Is that something you'd want?"

If the member is interested, they start shadowing that officer in March and April. Shadowing means: they're cc'd on relevant communications, they sit with the officer during meetings, and the officer explains what decisions they're making and why.

---

### April — Elections

**Nominations open at the first April meeting.**

Any active member in good standing can be nominated (including self-nomination). Secretary collects nominations over one week.

**Voting:**
- All active members vote
- Current officers vote
- Co-Presidents do not vote except to break a tie
- Results announced at the second April meeting

**What the incoming officer receives on election:**
1. A congratulations from the outgoing officer
2. A date for their first transition meeting
3. Access to the relevant section of this OS

---

### May — Transition Period

The four weeks in May are the overlap period. Old officers still run things. New officers are present for every decision.

**Transition meeting format (one per role):**
Old officer and new officer meet for 45–60 minutes. The old officer covers:

1. What this role actually does week to week (not what the job description says — what you actually do)
2. What's on your plate right now and what needs to happen before June
3. The top 3 things you wish you'd known when you started
4. Relationships that matter (who is helpful, who is tricky, who to call when something breaks)
5. Open question time

**After the transition meeting:**
Old officer writes a one-page transition document (template below). New officer receives it within one week.

---

### June — Full Handoff

By the last week of school:

- All shared accounts (social media, GitHub org, financial accounts) have been transferred to the incoming officers
- All shared documents have been moved to a location the new leadership controls
- Old officers have been removed from or demoted in any admin accounts they no longer need
- New Co-Presidents have had an introductory email exchange with Mr. Novak

Old officers are available by phone or text through the summer for questions. But the club is no longer their responsibility. It belongs to the new leadership.

---

## Transition Document Template

Every outgoing officer completes this before their last meeting:

```
ROLE: [Role Name]
OUTGOING OFFICER: [Name]
INCOMING OFFICER: [Name]
DATE: [Month, Year]

WHAT I ACTUALLY DO EACH WEEK:
[2–4 bullet points. Be honest about what takes time.]

RECURRING TASKS AND WHEN THEY HAPPEN:
[List with rough timing — "first week of October," "day before every meeting," etc.]

TOOLS AND ACCOUNTS I USE:
[List with login info or instructions on where to find credentials]

CONTACTS I RELY ON:
[Name, role, what they help with, how to reach them]

WHAT WENT WELL THIS YEAR:
[2–3 things. Be specific.]

WHAT I WOULD DO DIFFERENTLY:
[2–3 things. Be honest.]

THINGS TO WATCH OUT FOR:
[Problems that came up this year that the incoming officer should know about]

ONE PIECE OF ADVICE:
[One thing. The most important thing you know about this role that isn't written anywhere else.]
```

This document is stored in the club's shared drive permanently. It is not deleted when the person graduates. It is the institutional memory.

---

## What Happens If Succession Fails

Sometimes a graduating senior holds a role no one else can fill. This happens. Here is the response:

**Option A: Merge the role.** Two smaller roles are handled by one existing officer for the year. The role is reconstructed properly in the following year.

**Option B: Interim appointment.** A strong member is appointed to the role on a trial basis for one semester, with the explicit understanding that it will be voted on again in November.

**Option C: The role goes unfilled.** Some roles (like a second Secretary or second Treasurer) can be handled by one person for a year. Document that it's happening, note that it's not ideal, and recruit specifically for that role at the start of the following year.

**What never happens:** The outgoing officer holds onto control because no one else knows how to do it. That is the exact failure mode this entire protocol exists to prevent.

## Alumni Network

Every graduating member is an asset. Not a liability. Not a stranger.

**What alumni can do:**
- Guest present at a meeting on what they've been building in college or their career
- Advise teams remotely (Discord, email)
- Connect teams with contacts, internships, or competitions
- Donate to the club fund

**What the club does for alumni:**
- Maintains a contact list (name, email, graduation year, where they went, what they're doing)
- Sends a one-paragraph "here's what the club is doing this year" update each September
- Invites them to the April showcase

The alumni network compounds over time. Ten years from now, this club will have former members at companies, universities, and startups who can do things for current students that no one at the school can do for them. Tend that network.
