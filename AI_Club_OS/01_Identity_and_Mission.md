# 01 — Identity and Mission

---

## What We Are

The Northern Highlands AI Club is a building club. Not a learning club. Not a discussion club. A building club.

Every serious member is working on something. A project, a tool, a prototype, an entry to a competition. The meetings exist to accelerate that work — not to replace it. Students who come to watch but never build are welcome to observe. They are not in good standing.

This distinction matters because it shapes everything: how we recruit, how we present ourselves, what we put in announcements, and how we evaluate whether a meeting was successful.

---

## Mission Statement

**To develop the next generation of builders by giving high school students access to production-grade AI tools, real project experience, and a competitive environment that demands they ship.**

Not "to teach students about AI." Not "to explore the implications of artificial intelligence." To build. To ship. To compete.

---

## What We Build

Members of this club build things that work in the real world:

- Web applications with live AI integrations (APIs, not demos)
- Automation systems that solve real problems
- Competition entries that go to national judges
- Tools that other students actually use

The benchmark is always: would this work outside of a school setting? If the answer is yes, it belongs here.

---

## Our Positioning

**We are the most technically serious student organization in the school.**

We don't compete with the robotics team or the coding club on their terms. We operate at a different level — production code, real APIs, national competitions, tools that people actually use.

When we recruit, this positioning is the filter. We want students who are curious enough to stay up late figuring something out. We don't want students who want to put "AI Club" on a college application without doing the work.

---

## How We Talk About Ourselves

**In announcements and posts:**
- Direct and specific. Name the demo. Name the person. Describe what was built.
- Human tone. Reads like a text from a person, not a press release.
- No em dashes. No corporate language. No "we are pleased to announce."

**In emails to administration:**
- Professional but not stiff. Get to the point in the first line.
- Frame everything in terms of student outcomes and competitive advantage for the school.
- Ask for specific things. Don't ask for support "in general."

**In competition submissions:**
- Let the work speak. Describe what you built, why you built it, and what it does.
- No inflated language. Judges have seen a thousand inflated submissions. Precision wins.

**In conversations with parents:**
- These are the same tools used by engineers at Google, Anthropic, and OpenAI.
- Their student is building a portfolio, not just attending a club.
- Every dollar donated goes directly to a project team, not overhead.

---

## Core Values

**Ship or learn why you didn't.**
Every sprint ends with something to show. If it doesn't, the team owes an explanation of what blocked them and how they're removing the blocker. Stalling without explanation is not acceptable.

**Specificity over hype.**
"I built a canvas integration that pulls live assignment data and generates personalized study plans" is better than "I built an AI tutoring app." Be precise about what you did and how it works.

**Credit goes where it's earned.**
We don't inflate anyone's role. We don't give leadership credit to people who didn't lead. Competition entries accurately reflect who did the work.

**The club outlives its founders.**
Every decision is evaluated through this lens: will this still work in three years when none of the current leadership is here? If no, we do it differently.

---

## What Success Looks Like

At the end of the 2026–2027 academic year, the AI Club is successful if:

1. At least 3 project teams submitted to a national competition
2. Room 133 had consistent open lab attendance (not just meeting day traffic)
3. A new officer cohort was elected and fully onboarded before seniors graduated
4. The club operated without requiring any single person to carry it
5. At least one member built something that reached users outside the school
