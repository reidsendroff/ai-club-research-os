# 05 — Project Teams

---

## Why Teams Exist

Individual projects are fine. Team projects are better — not because collaboration is inherently good, but because the best problems require more than one person to solve them competitively, and because team accountability creates pressure that solo work doesn't.

Every active member is on a team. No exceptions. If a member is working on a solo project, they're still assigned to a team and present with that team at scrums.

---

## Team Structure

**Size:** 3–4 members per team. Not 2, not 5. 2 isn't enough to cover a build; 5 creates diffused accountability.

**Duration:** Teams stay together for the full academic year. No reshuffling mid-year except for extraordinary circumstances (member leaves the club, serious interpersonal conflict that VP/COO cannot resolve).

**Composition:** Each team should ideally have at least one member who can build something (code, design, API integration) and at least one member who can communicate it (present, write, explain). Teams that are all builders and no communicators struggle at competitions. Teams that are all communicators and no builders have nothing to communicate.

---

## How Teams Form

**September, during the first two weeks of the year:**

1. VP/COO collects interest forms from all members (what you've built, what you want to build, what tools you know)
2. Co-Presidents and VP/COO draft team assignments based on skill fit, interest fit, and balance
3. Assignments are announced at the Week 3 meeting
4. Teams have one week to raise serious objections. After that, assignments are final.

**For new members joining mid-year:**
VP/COO places them on the team with the most relevant project and the best fit for their skill level. This happens within two weeks of their joining.

---

## Team Roles

Every team has two assigned roles:

**Team Lead** — One person. Owns the scrum presentation, coordinates with their VP/COO liaison, and makes final decisions when the team disagrees on direction. Does not have to be the best technical person on the team — has to be the most organized.

**VP/COO Liaison** — One VP/COO is assigned to each team. Their job is to check in between meetings, help remove blockers, and flag to Co-Presidents if a team is stuck or off-track.

---

## How Teams Run

**Between meetings:**
Teams decide their own working schedule. Some teams meet daily on Discord. Some only collaborate during open lab. Both are fine as long as they have something to show at each scrum.

**At scrums:**
Team Lead presents unless they explicitly delegate to another team member. VP/COO liaison sits near the team and is the person who asks the follow-up question after their presentation.

**When a team is stuck:**
The team lead messages their VP/COO liaison. The liaison either helps directly or escalates to a Co-President or brings it to the next meeting's work block.

---

## Project Selection

Teams select one primary project at the start of the year. The project must meet three criteria:

1. **It builds something.** Not a research paper. Not a presentation about a topic. An artifact — code, a tool, an application, a system — that didn't exist before.

2. **It uses AI in a meaningful way.** Not "we used ChatGPT to write our README." The AI is a core part of the project's function.

3. **It has a realistic scope.** Something that can be meaningfully progressed every two weeks, not something that requires 6 months of work before it does anything.

**Project approval:** Team Lead submits a one-paragraph description to VP/COO. VP/COO approves or asks for a scope adjustment. Co-Presidents are not involved in individual project approval — that's VP/COO's call.

---

## API Fund Distribution

Each team receives an equal share of the annual API fund at the start of Q2 (after projects are scoped and teams are stable).

**How it works:**
- Treasurer distributes a shared API key or pre-funded account to each team
- Teams track their own spending and report to the Treasurer monthly
- If a team exceeds their allocation, they submit a request to the Treasurer explaining why and what they'll use the additional funds for
- Over-allocation requests are approved by Co-Presidents, not Treasurer unilaterally

**Teams that underspend:** Remaining funds go back to the club pool at year end. There is no incentive to hoard — unused funds benefit future teams.

---

## Team Evaluation

Teams are evaluated at two points: mid-year (December) and end-of-year (April showcase).

**What's evaluated:**
- Consistency of scrum presentations (did they show up with something every time?)
- Technical ambition (did they try something hard?)
- Competition participation (did they enter anything?)
- Member development (did Year 1 members on this team grow?)

**What's NOT evaluated:**
- Whether the project "worked" in a commercial sense
- Lines of code
- Complexity of the tech stack

A team that tried something ambitious, hit walls, documented what they learned, and presented honestly every two weeks is a successful team. A team that built a simple CRUD app and won a competition is also a successful team. Different paths, both valid.

---

## When a Team Isn't Working

Signs a team is in trouble (VP/COO should escalate when they see two or more of these):
- Two consecutive scrums with nothing to show
- Team lead stops responding to liaison messages
- Team members are attending meetings but not sitting together or collaborating
- One person is doing all the work and the rest are passengers

**Intervention steps:**
1. VP/COO liaison has a direct 1:1 with the team lead. What's actually going on?
2. If it's a skill gap: connect the team with a stronger member or officer who can unblock them
3. If it's a motivation gap: have an honest conversation about whether this project is the right one
4. If it's an interpersonal problem: Co-President gets involved, team may be restructured
5. If nothing works: team is dissolved, members are redistributed, documented in the records
