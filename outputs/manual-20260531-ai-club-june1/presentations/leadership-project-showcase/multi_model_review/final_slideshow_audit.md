## Verdict

Do not present this version as-is.

The strategic concept is right: June 15 should be framed as proof of work, not a popularity contest. The deck correctly moves toward evaluating grit, AI/build skill, and presentation/personable skill. But the current 29-slide version is still too long, still has vague logistics, and still carries language that makes the leadership selection feel more like a tryout than a serious club operating system.

This can be fixed surgically. The target should be a 22-slide deck, maximum 24. The deck needs fewer competition slides, clearer June 15 instructions, a Project Lead role, a warmer leadership tone, and a concrete submission deadline.

## What Works

- The deck has the right core frame: next year needs builders, operators, communicators, and people who follow through.
- The new June 15 proof-of-work concept is much stronger than picking leadership from vague applications.
- The three evaluation signals are correct:
  - grit
  - AI/coding/vibe-coding skill
  - presentation and personable skill
- The role-specific slides are useful and should stay.
- The design style is coherent and still matches the original deck.
- The green line artifact appears removed in the rendered contact sheet.

## Blocking Issues

### 1. No clear demo time limit

Students need to know exactly how long they get. Without this, June 15 becomes chaotic.

Use this rule:

> Each applicant gets up to 5 minutes total: 4 minutes to demo, 1 minute for questions. If more than 15 people apply, we switch to 3-minute lightning demos and publish the order ahead of time.

### 2. Submission logistics are not specific enough

The deck says to submit through Canvas, but does not name the module, deadline, or what must be submitted.

Add one dedicated logistics slide:

> Submit in Canvas module: June 15 Leadership Project Showcase  
> Deadline: Saturday, June 13, 2026 at 11:59 PM  
> Upload: project link, short demo video, resume, and role choices  
> Demo on June 15: 4-minute project walkthrough + 1-minute Q&A  
> No slides required. Show the real project.

If the new leadership form web app is used instead of Canvas, replace the Canvas line with the Railway URL once it is live.

### 3. Slide 7 still says "tryout"

This word is wrong for the tone. It sounds theatrical and exclusionary. The right phrase is:

> June 15 is the proof-of-work showcase.

or:

> June 15 is where we see who builds.

### 4. Project Lead is missing

The club plan depends on project teams. A leadership deck that does not include Project Lead creates a structural gap.

Add a dedicated Project Lead slide:

> Project Lead  
> Owns one team project from idea to demo. Keeps the team moving, divides work, solves blockers, and makes sure the project actually ships. This role is for people who can build and help other people build.

### 5. The deck is too long

29 slides is too much for tomorrow's meeting, especially with the leadership section, tools section, competition updates, and questions. Students will remember the core ask only if the deck is sharper.

Target: 22 slides. Hard cap: 24 slides.

### 6. Competition section overwhelms the leadership message

Slides 21-27 currently pull attention away from the June 15 leadership mechanism. The competition section should be one summary slide, maybe two if absolutely necessary.

Recommended one-slide competition copy:

> Competitions next year  
> Congressional App Challenge, USAAIO, IAI2O, Presidential AI Challenge, MIT Blueprint, FBLA, and local hackathons.  
> The point is not to sign up for everything. The point is to build strong projects early enough that we can choose the right competitions instead of scrambling at the deadline.

### 7. Role count is unclear

The screenshot says "5 seats," but the operating plan mentions an 8-officer structure plus possible project leads. This must be reconciled before presenting.

Recommended phrasing:

> Core roles: VP/COO, CMO, Treasurer, Secretary, TA/Admin  
> Project Lead roles: selected based on the number and quality of project teams  
> We care more about fit and follow-through than filling a fixed number of titles.

If there really are only 5 seats, say that clearly and remove the 8-officer language elsewhere.

## Delivery Risk

The biggest risk is not the design. It is that students leave without knowing exactly what to do next.

Every student should be able to answer these before leaving:

1. What do I build?
2. Where do I submit it?
3. When is it due?
4. How long is my demo?
5. What are Reid and Ben judging?
6. What roles exist?

The current deck does not yet make all six answers impossible to miss.
